Curriculum for white Britons to educate ourselves on racism

This website is written by white people for white people as an introduction to the problem of racism.

Racism is a serious issue in our country and has been for a very long time. However, as white people we rarely experience racial prejudice and it can be all too easy to remain ignorant of the experiences of people from racialised minorities. It's time we made more of an effort to understand — and we can't expect people from racialised minorities to do the work to educate us.

Hopefully this website will help us better understand racism — and help us work towards becoming anti-racists and better allies with people from racialised minorities.

The topics listed below are designed to be studied in order, but skip about as you might need. Each article is less than two A4 pages. Before you begin, it might be worthwhile reading the note on terminology.

Overview

  1. Understanding race

    As racism is based on the incorrect notions that humanity is divided into distinct biological or cultural "races", we'll begin here. We'll see how, even though these distinctions don't exist, we still often classify people into groups — "races" — and structure our society around these classifications, privileging a certain group (i.e., ourselves: white people) over others.

  2. Understanding privilege

    Before we consider how society privileges us white people, we'll get some grounding by considering privilege more generally.

  3. Understanding White Privilege

    Now we should be more prepared to consider how British society privileges white people, giving us certain advantages that are not experienced by racialised minorities.

  4. Understanding racism

    Racism, we'll see, is the other side of the coin to white privilege: we are privileged at the expense of racialised minorities. Racism isn't only something individual white people are involved with: more importantly, it's in the structure of our society (evidenced by the existence of white privilege).

  5. Understanding white fragility

    All of this can be new — and uncomfortable — for us to hear. We might feel angry or upset. How we react now is important, so we'll consider why we feel this way before moving on to think about how we can respond better.

  6. Becoming an anti-racist

    Hopefully by now we'll have a better grasp of racism in the UK — and hopefully we'll want to do something about it, so we'll consider some steps we can take to become an anti-racist and an ally of racialised minorities.
