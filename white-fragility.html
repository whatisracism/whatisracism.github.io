Understanding white fragility

While we might experience challenges and hardship in our lives, as white people the colour of our skin isn’t a hindrance to us in society. When it comes to this aspect of our lives, we are comfortable. White people continue to be privileged by society.

Because it’s so unusual for us to be outside this racial comfort zone, when we’re challenged about it we can feel extremely uncomfortable and we can feel the need to get things back to how they normally are. As white people, we are very fragile in this way. It doesn’t take much to upset us about race. We become defensive, angry and distraught. We might claim we’re being victimised; that we’re experiencing “reverse racism”. We might say things like, “But I’m not a racist”; “But I’ve got Black friends”; “Well, I’m “coloured blind” - I don’t see race”; “I was taught to see everyone the same”. We might become distraught and require comforting. We might remove ourselves from the conversation; just walk away. All of these responses are defensive and deflective. They are an attempt to remove race from the topic of conversation. And they are an attempt to keep the status quo: a situation in which we are comfortable because society privileges white people at the expense of racialised minorities. We’ve lost an opportunity to learn and develop.

When we react from our fragility like this, we make it so miserable for people from racialised minorities to talk to us about racism that often they don’t. It can be exhausting - and futile - for people from racialised minorities to do so when it’s met with this childish reaction and resistance.

Of course, none of this has to be intentional; it can be unconscious. But it’s really unhelpful and damaging. Insidiously, not only does white fragility stop any moments of positive development for white people, the response of white fragility functions as a form of white racial control, further disempowering people from racialised minorities, holding back positive change.

We can learn to be more resilient and not react in such a fragile way when racism is pointed out. We can learn to see such moments as opportunities to learn and develop. This is one of the first steps towards becoming anti-racist.
